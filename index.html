About the case study

Bellabeat is a high-tech manufacture of health-focused products aimed at women.

The idea for this case study is to take publicly available FitBit data and see if there are potential ways to guide the advertisement of products from Bellabeat, either to improve the experience of current users or to potentially find a space in the market whose needs have not yet been met.

The main focus for recommendations is going to be around finding marketing suggestions for their mainline product the Ivy. The Ivy is a smart connected health tracker that stands out as a product made to look like a fashion piece first. It can be purchased with a variety of accessories that lets the Ivy be worn as jewelry on the wrist, around the neck, or a multitude of other options to integrate with the users everyday life.

Data used for this case study can be found on this Kaggle page. Licensing information for the data used can be found here.

Questions to answer

  1. What is the user base for FitBit like? What potential market segments are missing?
  2. What features arent being fully utilized by users and why?
  3. How could users utilize Ivy to alleviate potential issues with FitBit?

However, the majority of users are tracking enough day to day which suggests that the majority of users do wear their fitness trackers daily and like to use the heart rate tracking function.

Low sleep tracking usage

Next were going to try and see if we can figure out why users are quitting sleep tracking after only a few tries.

sleepPerDay %>% 
  ggplot(aes(SleepDay)) +
  geom_bar(aes(fill = n %in% c(1:15))) +
  labs(title = "Low Participation in Sleep Tracking",
       subtitle = "Looking for trends in users quitting",
       x = "Date",
       fill = "Low Participation")

So looking at the overall trends there isnt a early adoption drop off. But there are spikes and and then valleys overall within the low participation users, suggesting that users are picking up and dropping the feature quickly. Only that this may happen later into using the device for some. To be sure we need to look at each users daily trends individually.

sleepPerDay %>%
  filter(n < 15) %>% 
  ggplot(aes(SleepDay, factor(Id))) +
  geom_point(aes(color = factor(Id), size = 20)) +
  theme(legend.position = "none") +
  labs(title = "Sleep Tracking by Day Per User, Low Utilization Only",
       subtitle = "Each dot is a day where sleep tracking was utilized",
       x = "Date",
       y = "User")

The majority of low participation users are picking up and dropping sleep tracking fairly quickly. Most individual users that didnt use much sleep tracking tried it for a couple of days and missed a day or two before trying again before giving up entirely. This suggests that there is something they run into that makes them quit using the feature. This could be a couple of things. 1. The user tries sleep tracking and finds out they arent interested in the data they get 2. The user isnt getting relevant recommendations and stops using the feature 3. The device is interfering with their quality of sleep and they stop wearing it when they go to bed

This could an area where Ivy is poised to fill a need. The Ivy being a smaller device that easily attached to a multitude of other wearable items means that it could handle the discomfort factor quiet easily. However, there isnt enough data to sort this possibility out from the rest. Before moving forward with this as an advertising angle I would recommend further studies, but that will be covered later.

Sleep tracking usage

Next up is the users who did use sleep tracking consistently. Two things to look out for with sleep quality is both how long, 6-8 hours is recommeded depending on age and other factors, but also sleep consistency. A range of sleep times can mean that someone is getting poor quality sleep even if they are getting the right amount the majority of the time.

sleepPerDayFiltered %>% 
  ggplot(aes(TotalMinutesAsleep)) +
  geom_density(aes(alpha = 0.1, fill = factor(Id), color = factor(Id))) +
  facet_wrap(~factor(Id)) +
  scale_x_time(position = "top") +
  theme(legend.position = "none") +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45)) +
  labs(title = "Time Spent Asleep by User",
       subtitle = "Spread in sleep times may indicate poor sleep quality",
       x = "Hours Asleep")

Looking at the density spread you would want to see a close bell curve near that 6-8 hour mark, indicating that someone is consistently getting the right amount of sleep. Very few of the users here show that. Some have some very small spots near the low end, usually meaning naps, but a lot are spread flat meaning that they are sleeping a wide range of times.

Bellabeats app could utilize this data to send recommendations to users about getting more consistent sleep times or sending a reminder to put down electronic devices a set time before a pre-determined sleep schedule. Both helping the user get more consistent sleep while also making the data collected useful to the user, making them want to continue using it.

This data is leaning towards showing that the looks of the device matter as much to the user, sometimes even more given the situation, as its functionality. But we need to dig deeper to make sure thats actually true.

You can quickly see which users take off the FitBit at night as well as the one who mostly uses it for exercise. There are a few different types of trends to look for with the higher heart rates. * Clusters around 5PM to 8PM, indicating regular exercise or more active time immediately after getting out of work * Spreads of high heart rates over the afternoons into early mornings indicating regular use, but only 2 of note there * Overall wide spread high heart rates indicating a more active lifestyle overall or a physically active job

So while there is some activity suggesting that some people wear their FitBit at all times, the number of people is very few. And if we take a look at overall trends looking for weekdays vs.weekends again it shows a similar outlook.

heartRateHourly %>%

Again we can see a large clustering later in the day, when most people get off work, implying exercise or simply going out right after clocking out. Interestingly the weekend contributes to the majority of the mid day spike before 5PM. Most important for our use, most of the high heart readings for after midnight are from Saturday and Sunday, meaning those are people going out later at night on their time off. But comparing those measurements with the previous chart, those are covered by only 2 of the people being measured. Altogether this means that lack of data on weekends is coming from both leaving the device off until later in the day and not wearing the device in the afternoon. Suggesting that a good missing market segment could be reached by aiming to show how the Ivy is poised to fit more into users weekend life.

Recommendations and potential unknowns

Looking at what we can find in this data the biggest focus for the advertising direction of the Ivy should lean on its ability to fit in as a fashion piece alongside its metrics. There is a noticeably under-served part of the market that does like using the FitBit to track their data, but doesnt wear it on the weekends or out late, and a big portion could be the lack of ability to fit the device in with their lifestyle. Focusing on how the device fits into a wide range of needs and styles to match the changes of a users day could help to bridge that need for customers.

There is also the possibility of this device being much more comfortable and usable for sleep tracking, its clear that users are interested in utilizing this feature, but drop off for one reason or another. However, there isnt enough data here to make a definitive conclusion on why they drop off and if this was a pursued angle I would suggest further research be done.

Potential future research

When looking into another advertisement run I think it would be beneficial to add in the focus on sleep tracking to really push the Ivy as a device that fits into all parts of ones lifestyle. But to gather more data to figure out what customers do feel is missing from current sleep tracking I think a customer survey would help a lot. A lot of potential issues with sleep tracking would be classified as qualitative data and surveys are a good spot to let people fill out these concerns and issues. Then once a market portion has been pinned down that is interested in sleep tracking but is missing a need that Ivy can provide, then it would be a good idea to move forward with adding that to future campaigns.

There is also the possibility of integrating more sleep tracking uses like the electronics down reminder or having the user set a standard sleep time for them to aim for that could be implemented by the dev team to give more uses to the sleep tracking data already being provided to users. This could also feed back into future advertising campaigns.
